Problem Solutions

The Sun

Converts sunlight to electricity using solar photovoltaic (PV) or concentrating solar-thermal power (CSP). PV's are generally used in solar panels.

• PV: When light photons hit the PV cell, they can either reflect, pass through, or get absorbed by the cell. Only the absorbed photons generate electricity. When the semiconductor material in the cell absorbs a certain amount of photons, they become excited and start moving, generating electricity.

• CSP: Light reflects off mirrors and is directed to a small area (receiver), usually located at the top of a tall tower. Inside the receiver, a fluid is heated which can reach up to 500 degrees celsius. The hot fluid is then transferred to water which produces steam to spin turbines.

The Wind

Wind turbines use wind’s kinetic energy to spin the blades to create electricity

• Utility Scale: Turbines that exceed 100 kilowatts in size and are typically installed in large wind farms. They tend to be more cost effective and are grouped in bulks to provide more power to the electrical grid

• Offshore Wind: Turbines that tend to be massive and are installed to the seabed in water depths of 20-60 meters. These turbines harness the kinetic energy of ocean currents, generating electricity that is transmitted through cables to onshore facilities.

• Distributed Wind: Wind energy generation that occurs on a smaller scale, typically closer to where electricity is used. Distributed wind systems are installed closer to homes, business, and communities. They can be installed on roofs, backyards, or small parcels of land, and can be connected to power grids. However, owners of the turbines can be compensated for providing to the grid.

The Water

Harnesses the energy of natural flowing water to generate electricity.

• Impoundment: A large hydropower system uses a dam to store river water in a reservoir. Water released from the reservoir flows through a turbine, spinning it, which in turn activates a generator to produce electricity. The water can be released in different amounts based on the changing electricity needs.

• Diversion: Sometimes called a “run-of-river” facility, channels a portion of a river through a canal and/or a penstock (a closed conduit that channels the flow of water to turbines) to utilize the natural decline of the river elevation to produce energy. A diversion may not require the use of a dam.

• Pumped Storage: Using other power sources, such as solar or wind, PSH (pumped storage hydropower) uses that energy to pump water from a reservoir at a lower elevation to a reservoir at a higher elevation during times of low electricity demand. However, when the demand for electricity increases, the stored water in the upper reservoir is released back to the lower reservoir, spinning turbines to conduct electricity (this part is similar to diversion).

Geothermal

Draws fluids from underground reservoirs at the Earth’s crust to the surface which turns the heat of the fluid to steam, driving turbines.

• Dry Steam: Are power plants that utilize hydrothermal fluids which are predominantly steam. The steam is directly channeled to turbines to generate electricity. When the steam condenses it is directed back into the underground reservoir to be used again (oldest type).

• Flash Stream: Fluids at temperatures greater than 182°C/360°F, pumped from deep underground, are transferred from an area of high pressure to low pressure. The pressure difference causes some of the fluid to rapidly condense or “flash” into steam, which can then be used to turn turbines. If any liquid is not transformed, it is injected back underground to be used in a second “flash”.

• Binary-Cycle: Generates electricity by using the hot fluid to heat a secondary fluid with a lower boiling point. The secondary fluid vaporizes at a lower temperature than water so it doesn’t need extremely high temperatures to turn into steam. The steam is then used to drive a turbine. This allows power plants to generate electricity from lower-temperature geothermal resources.
